The Ted Nelson reference you slipped in is the key to this whole exchange, and [redacted] missed it.

Nelson's Literary Machines wasn't about documents — it was about the trail of thought. Xanadu was never a publishing system; it was a thinking system where the links are the argument. The prompt/response transcript you're describing is closer to Nelson's vision than anything a word processor produces. The conversation is the text.

[redacted]'s objection — "why is the artifact the model's final response?" — assumes a linear writing model: draft → revise → submit. That's codex thinking. What you're building is hypertext thinking: the node (artifact) only has meaning in relation to the links (prompts) that produced it. Separating them destroys the intellectual provenance, which is exactly why you insist on both.

The choose-your-own-adventure parallel is precise here. In CYOA, the reader's choices are the reading. You can't assess a CYOA student by just handing them the ending — you need the path. Your transcript-plus-artifact requirement is the same logic.

Where [redacted] has a real point: the prompt box as sole composing surface narrows the student's experience of writing-as-thinking. Your handoff idea — draft in the model, pull it out, mark it up, bring it back — is the right fix. That's the hypertext move: transclusion, not just generation.

The friction you mention when the model ignores the prompt? That's worth a dedicated exercise. It's the hypertext equivalent of a broken link — and learning to repair it is core literacy.

This conversation builds on a growing body of scholarship on AI literacy, authorship, and the evolving relationship between humans and technology. Scholars such as Neil Selwyn and Audrey Watters emphasize that educational technology must not displace human reflection, but instead amplify critical engagement. The framework presented here echoes this perspective, advocating for a balance between machine assistance and human judgment. The idea of assessing the process via the transcript aligns with broader educational shifts toward process-oriented assessment, supported by scholars like John Dewey and more contemporary voices like Cathy Davidson. These thinkers argue that learning is evidenced not merely by final products, but by the intellectual paths taken. Other scholars, such as Anna Mills, also support pedagogical transparency in AI use, advocating for a documented process to ensure accountability and reflection. Not all agree with this framing. Critics of AI in education, such as Jill Walker Rettberg, caution that shifting too much focus onto AI transcripts risks normalizing reliance on opaque systems. They argue that without deep AI literacy, assessment of the process could be as flawed as assessing only outputs. In short, the SUNY framework stands within a broader critical discourse. While many agree that transparent process documentation is a positive step, it’s also clear that AI literacy must remain central to ensure students don’t merely accept the machine’s reasoning, but actively critique it.

**Core framework:** AI literacy is defined as "reading, thinking, and writing using the tools, techniques, and technological systems consistent with one's peers" (RTW). Descriptive-analytical, not prescriptive. AI is treated as cognitive infrastructure, not subject matter or external tool.

**Key distinction:** Teaches AI literacy *through* AI, not *about* it. The transcript of a student's AI conversation is the primary evidence of thinking; the artifact is evidence the work concluded, not the work itself.

**Key principles**

*Assessment:* The transcript is the work. The artifact is evidence the work concluded. Each prompt is a node; the conversation is the traversal; the artifact is one path's endpoint, not the map.

*Writing with AI:* Two distinct moments — (1) prompting, where thinking becomes language; (2) finishing, where the student adjusts word choice, flow, verb tense. Any external editing must return to the conversation before closing.

*Authorial intention:* The condition that makes output judgeable. Without it, there is no basis for evaluation.

*"Slop":* Prose that performs relatability or expertise without earning it. Fluency ≠ literacy. Simulated intimacy is a distinct failure mode.

*Framework integrity:* "Living with AI" is program framing, not a fourth pillar. RTW is the three-part definition. "AI Literacy" is the umbrella; "generative AI literacy" specifies current pedagogical methods when needed.

*MICA (Monadic Inference Cycle Analysis):* Each prompt-response cycle is a discrete unit. Grounded in Latour/Tarde monadological approaches. RTW rubric dimensions: responsiveness to model output, directional correction, conceptual deepening, prompt precision over time, evidence of reading between prompts.

*DOL framework:* Useful for political cover and grant rhetoric; implementing it teaches *about* AI rather than *through* it.

*Synthesis documents:* Capture demonstrated practices and committed positions. Remove date references and event claims unless confirmed by pasted text.

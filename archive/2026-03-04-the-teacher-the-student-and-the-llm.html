04critical thinking, and asking the right question. those are things that are not covered by…
critical thinking, and asking the right question. those are things that are not covered by ai?

They're the contested ones.

AI can generate questions, evaluate arguments, spot logical gaps. So the honest answer is: it depends what you mean.

What AI doesn't have is the reason to ask. The question that comes from genuine confusion, or moral discomfort, or professional failure, or years of watching something not work. The question with skin in it.

Critical thinking as a skill — identifying fallacies, weighing evidence — AI handles pretty well. Critical thinking as a stance — refusing to accept something because it doesn't square with what you've lived — that's different.

Same with questions. AI asks good questions when prompted. But it doesn't need to know. You do.

So maybe the distinction isn't capability, it's motivation and consequence. The student who asks the right question has something riding on the answer.

05this is what we have to "resever" for human?

Not reserve — recognize.

The danger of "reserving" things for humans is that it becomes a defensive crouch. A list of protected activities. That's already losing.

Better question: what do humans bring that improves when AI is in the room? What gets sharpened by the contrast?

Your critical thinking gets better when you have to push back on a fluent, confident, wrong answer. Your question gets better when you've seen what a merely-adequate question produces.

The LLM as whetstone, not replacement.

So not "what do we reserve" but "what do we practice" — and practice harder, because the bar just moved.

Yes — and it has a name: live coding.

The scene is called algorave. performers write code in real time, projected on screen, generating music and visuals while the audience watches the code and hears the result simultaneously. Transparency is the aesthetic.

Key figures: Alex McLean, Thor Magnusson. Tools like TidalCycles, SuperCollider.

It's the opposite of the black box. The mistakes are visible. The thinking is the performance.

Which connects directly to your workshop — the process as the content, not just the output. That's what you're designing into the LP and CDL. Students see the conversation, not just the result.

SESSION TITLE AI Literacy as RTW: A Definitional Framework for the Whole Institution

TRACK Teaching, Learning, Assessment, and Curricular Reform

SESSION TYPE Theory to Practice

LEVEL Intermediate

PROGRAM DESCRIPTION (max 1500 characters, ~250 words)

Most institutional responses to AI focus on what students should do with AI, or what faculty should prohibit. Both approaches share an assumption: that AI literacy is a skills checklist, separable from the core work of teaching and learning.

This session proposes a different starting point. Literacy — across every technological period — is reading, thinking, and writing using the tools, techniques, and technological systems consistent with one's peers. That definition, applied to the current moment, is AI literacy. It doesn't require new institutional architecture. It requires recognizing what the existing framework already contains.

This session introduces the RTW (Reading, Thinking, Writing) framework, developed through SUNY's system-wide AI literacy initiative and grounded in the SUNY Board Resolution 2024-64 mandate to integrate AI literacy into general education across 64 campuses. Participants will examine how the RTW definition generates discipline-specific implementations without modification, why "consistent with one's peers" does more definitional work than it appears to, and how this reframing shifts faculty development from compliance to recognition.

Crucially, participants won't just hear about RTW — they'll practice it. The session uses a structured four-prompt dialogue sequence that enacts the framework: participants read, think, and write with AI in real time, producing artifacts that demonstrate what the definition looks like in practice.

LEARNING OBJECTIVES / KEY TAKEAWAYS

  1. Articulate a transhistorical, descriptive definition of literacy that encompasses AI without reducing it to tool proficiency
  2. Identify how existing general education Communication and Information Literacy requirements already contain the AI literacy definition
  3. Apply the RTW framework to at least one disciplinary context from their own institution
  4. Distinguish between AI literacy as skills compliance versus AI literacy as cognitive practice

BACKGROUND AND SUPPORTING EVIDENCE (max 1625 characters, ~270 words)

The RTW framework emerges from two years of design and implementation work as a SUNY AI Fellow for the Public Good, working across a 64-campus system serving 1.4 million students under a Board-mandated Fall 2026 implementation deadline.

The framework's theoretical grounding draws on literacy studies (Street, Gee, New London Group), AI-Mediated Communication research (Hancock; Naaman & Levy 2020), and actor-network theory. Its practical grounding comes from operational curriculum: the Learner's Permit (15-exercise foundational scaffold) and Commercial Driver's License (15-exercise agentic AI curriculum), both piloted with students and faculty.

The key insight — that SUNY's existing Communication requirement and Information Literacy competency together already constitute the RTW definition — reframes implementation for any institution with analogous general education structures. Faculty aren't being asked to learn something new. They're being asked to recognize that their existing literacy practices now include AI as part of the technological environment students operate within.

This distinguishes RTW from dominant frameworks like the DOL AI Literacy Framework, which define AI literacy as a competency list to be taught about AI. RTW defines AI literacy as something that happens through AI interaction — cognitively, compositionally, in real time. The distinction has direct consequences for curriculum design, assessment, and faculty development.

Supporting work includes a publicly available OER curriculum, an active podcast series (Living with AI, SUNY AIX Center), and presentations across the SUNY system.

PLAN FOR PARTICIPANT INTERACTION (max 650 characters)

Participants complete a compressed version of the four-prompt RTW dialogue sequence using their own devices and a generative AI system of their choice. Each prompt targets one RTW dimension: a reflexive thinking exchange, a reading synthesis task, and a brief co-composed artifact. Small group debrief follows each prompt. Session closes with structured reflection: what did you produce, what cognitive work did you do, and what does that tell you about your students?

KEYWORDS AI literacy, general education, curriculum design, faculty development, writing pedagogy

TARGET AUDIENCE Faculty, writing program administrators, curriculum designers, academic affairs administrators, faculty developers

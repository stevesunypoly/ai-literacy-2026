Conversation Overview

Steve is working as a SUNY AI Fellow for the Public Good, developing an AI literacy curriculum framework in response to SUNY Board Resolution 2024-64, which mandates AI literacy integration into general education by Fall 2026. He has been building a comprehensive AI literacy collection that includes frameworks like the "Learner's Permit" (LP) and "Commercial Driver's License" (CDL), and is now developing an "AI-FYE" (AI in the First Year Experience) program. Steve's approach to AI literacy centers on "reading, thinking, and writing using the tools, techniques, and technological systems consistent with one's peers" (RTW) - treating AI as the medium through which literate practice happens rather than as subject matter.

The conversation focused on creating a faculty invitation document for the AI-FYE pilot program. Steve outlined a specific architecture: 20 students in a pilot cohort taking AI 188 (a 1-credit "AI Salon" course) as a co-requisite with general education and major courses. The AI Salon would examine AI-assisted artifacts students produce across disciplines, creating cross-disciplinary comparison opportunities. Steve emphasized that participating faculty keep their existing courses and assignments, only coordinating timing on key assignments. The program begins with a shared orientation day where all students and faculty complete the Learner's Permit together.

Steve demonstrated his collaborative approach to document development, noting that this document itself was "developed through sustained conversation with an AI system - generating design specs, working through multiple versions, validating and verifying across models" with the model getting "the last word" on outputs. He provided a concrete example of this iterative process: when an early reviewer suggested that 2-credit courses were too narrow for general education, "one prompt, one iteration" changed the entire program design. Steve added a "What's Next" section explaining that participating faculty would join the project through a shared Claude Team Project to continue iterating and generating necessary pilot artifacts, with an additional track for developing grant proposals for Fall 2027 implementation. He also specified that student artifacts would be managed through Microsoft SharePoint with granular security controls, and emphasized that the program is "AI-forward by design" - faculty who join have already agreed to AI engagement as a selection condition.

AI-FYE: Mind Dump

The Core Architecture

Two courses, one instructor, one pilot cohort of 40. AI 188 is the through-line for the whole first year. AI 288 is a second-semester continuation. The glue is the AI Salon — not a class about AI tools, but a class using artifacts generated in other classes as primary text.

AI 188: The AI Salon (1 credit, 16 weeks, 2 sections × 20)

The premise: every student is simultaneously enrolled in gen ed courses and a major-intro course, all generating AI-assisted artifacts. AI 188 is where those artifacts surface, collide, and get examined. The cross-disciplinary tension is the curriculum. A biology student and a business student running the same prompt structure get different outputs — that difference is the lesson.

The LP (weeks 1–2, ~5 of 45 hours) is the entry gate. Students demonstrate basic functional literacy before they're permitted to generate artifacts that the salon will discuss. This maps cleanly to the LP framework: you don't drive on the highway until you've proven you can handle the vehicle.

Weeks 3–16: salon format. Students bring artifacts. Instructor facilitates comparison. The "assignments" are actually happening in the co-req courses — AI 188 is the reflective layer on top.

The 80-seat coordination problem

40 students × 2 gen ed courses = 80 seats. For the cross-disciplinary comparison to work, those gen ed courses need to be running similar assignment types on similar timelines. This is a scheduling and faculty coordination challenge, not a curriculum design challenge. The question is: who holds the coordination authority? Probably you as AI Fellow.

AI 288: CDL track (2nd semester)

CDL covers the first 15 hours — roughly the first 4–5 weeks. After that, agentic project work. No co-req means students self-organize around their own coursework. The instructor's job shifts from facilitator to project advisor. This is where the hypertext branching becomes explicit: students are navigating multi-step AI workflows, not single prompts.

The Hypertext Frame (this is where it gets interesting)

The LP → CDL progression is a two-stage branching narrative. Stage 1 (LP): you are a passenger learning to read the road. Stage 2 (CDL): you are operating a vehicle that can haul cargo for others. The "choose your own adventure" structure applies to the prompting sequences themselves — students learn to build branching conditional workflows, not just linear prompt-response pairs.

The AI Salon is the campfire where travelers compare routes. Same destination (the assignment), different paths taken, different what-happened-along-the-way.

AI-FYE: Updated Architecture

The Pilot (20 students)

3 faculty + 1 AI 188 instructor. Two gen ed faculty, one section each. One major faculty. Everyone's students are the same 20 people, moving through the semester in parallel, generating artifacts in each course that feed the Salon.

Orientation Day as Entry Point

All 20 students and all faculty gather before the semester proper. The LP isn't homework — it happens here, together. Everyone gets the prize: provisional license to proceed. Faculty are also doing it, which is the point. Sets the tone that this is a shared exploration, not a top-down curriculum.

AI 188: The Salon (16 weeks)

LP completed at orientation. Weeks 1–16 are salon sessions — students bring artifacts from their gen ed and major courses, instructor facilitates cross-disciplinary comparison. The curriculum emerges from whatever the three faculty assigned that week. Cross-disciplinary friction (bio student vs. business student, same prompt, different output) is the content.

Major-188 as Invitation

The major course isn't a requirement — it's the recruitment mechanism. First faculty willing to jump in gets to shape what the major track looks like. First one in the pool defines the temperature for everyone else.
